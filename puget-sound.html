Project Details

My Role: UX Researcher

Duration: 2 Weeks

Tools: Sketch, Illustrator

Teammates: Charlotte Hulseman - Project Manager, Leah Morales - Interaction Designer

Background

Puget Sound Trip Planner is a trip plannng app that allows users to plan trips, locate bus stops, and view departures within the Puget Sound region. However, with an average score of 1.9 out of 5, there was room for improvement in terms of design, features, and the overall experience. As the user researcher, my goal was to find out what kind of problems the app was facing and then work with the team to propose changes that would address these issues.

Reseach & Analysis

I conducted preliminary research on what users were saying about Trip Planner in the app store. Users cited numerous issues such as frequent crashing, unreliable bus time information, and broken features. However, users praised its unique alert feature that notifies users when bus routes are cancelled or when buses stop runnng due to weather, road work, etc. We discovered through competitive analysis that this alert feature is not seen in any other trip planning app.

Interviews & Affinity Mapping

I conducted six twenty-minute qualitative interviews with a set list of questions to kick off user discovery. Afterward our team explored the findings from the interviews to define the problem Trip Planner users were facing. We discovered through our findings that users used Trip Planner primarily to see if there are changes to their buses and bus routes, but used other trip planning apps for everything else because they were easier to use.

Persona

Madison Maley is our user persona. She is a 20 year old college student who uses multiple trip planning apps to get around the city and meet up with friends after school. She checks Trip Planner to get notification and alerts about her bus routes, but uses other apps to plan her trips. We can serve Madison by making Trip Planner more user-friendly so she only needs to rely on one app.

Problem & Hypothesis

Trip Planner was designed to be the go to trip planning app for the Puget Sound region. We have observed that the wide breadth of categories and disorganized content are causing distrust with users, leading them to turn away from it. How might we make the app more trustworthy and engaging for users?

We believe that reorganizing the structure and layout of Trip Planner so that it follows the design patterns of other reputable trip planning apps will create a familiar experience, resulting in better trust and engagement. We will know we're right when we see an increase in overall user satisfaction.

Design Approach

As a team we evaluated all of our research and prioritized the features we were going to address for improvement. Our interaction designer, Leah Morales developed the following wireframes to propose new solutions.

Map Home Screen & Tab Navigation

Our team created a map section on the home screen with tab bar navigation to reduce the amount of steps needed to access the map screen and other features.

Filters

The filters section was redesigned to allow users to quickly toggle between what they want to see and not see on the map.

Alerts & Notification Feature

The main appeal of the app - this feature was added in the route selection screen so that users could see notifications and alerts for that particular route.

Strategic Analysis

Users suggested that Puget Sound Trip Planner would be more intuitive if it followed some design patterns of their favorite apps. In response to this, my team took a look at competitors app layouts and paid close attention to the apps mentioned in user interviews (Google, Apple, and One Bus Away). My discovery was that these more popular apps had a map on the home screen as well as minimal navigation. Puget Sound Trip Planner has an unwelcoming interface and is not intuitive to use. The app has a map which allows to find your destination relative to where you are, but it took some additional navigation to get to that feature.

To find out better who my users were and what their needs and wants were, we went out and asked people near bus stops and public locations about what kind of mobile transportation app they used. These people ranged from students to working adults. Based on the research we collected, we discovered 6 out of 7 people we tested used 2 or more transportation apps for specific purposes. For example, people used Google Maps because it is easy and intuitive to find directions. However, at an actual bus stop, people would utilize One Bus Away because it provided them with real time information of when the bus would arrive. Google Maps does not always provide reliable or real time information.
