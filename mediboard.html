Project Details

My Role: UX Designer

Duration: 3 Months

Tools: Figma, Webflow, Notion, Pencil & Paper

Background

When it comes to medical information, they can be difficult to understand or look for. MediBoard is a data visualization tool to quantitatively compare medical treatments against conditions like depression, anxiety, and insomnia using real and up-to-date clinical trial data. As the designer, I conducted research, iterated & tested designs, and facilitated development of the app with developers.

Preliminary Research

Our team looked into the business viability of this tool by exploring existing medical databases and registries such as ClinicalTrials.gov and National Institutes of Health. In addition, we spoke to multiple people on social media sites and network groups like Reddit and Facebook.

Competitive Analysis

Our team created a competitive analysis chart to see what our competitors were doing. Based on this analysis, we discovered some common problems such as information being unorganized, out-of-date, and/or generally difficult to digest for the average user.

User Stories

Our team drafted three different user stories that aligned with the most common responses from the feedback we received. This exercise would help us ideate and arrive at the optimal solution for each problem.



I would like to know the treatment options for a condition:

  • User visits MediBoard
  • Searches for condition
  • Visits the condition page
  • Looks at all the possible treatments
  • Looks at recommendations for user posts
  • Compares treatments side-by-side


I would like to know the alternatives to this treatment:

  • User visits MediBoard
  • Searches for treatment
  • Looks at the alternatives treatments section
  • Browse and starts to compare them
  • Comes up with a few they like


I would like to connect with others who share their conditions:

  • User visits MediBoard
  • Searches for their treatment or uses the message board tags
  • Browses post

Card Sort

Our team conducted a card sorting activity to figure out the placement of categories using Optimal Card Sort.

Persona Development

Problem

The goal of every medical database is to inform people. However, people need up-to-date information so they know the correct treatment and risks. For people who research, practice, or use medicine, the main concern is the accuracy and reliability of information. People need accurate medical information because they become frustrated if they receive the wrong diagnosis or medication.

Solution

We believe by creating a data visualization tool which allows users to get accurate and reliable information will create a pleasant searching experience. We will know this to be true when we see an increase in satisfaction and ratings.

Design Sketches & Ideation

While the developers on our team started exploring existing APIs and coding the app, my co-designer and I worked on coming up with sketches and designs of the filter categories and site info for prototype testing.

Filter Categories

Filter categories enable the user to quickly toggle between what they want to see on the map. We chose icons that best represented basic human needs - food, clothes, shelter, and medication with the soup (soup kitchen), T-shirt, house, and health cross images respectively.

Site Info

Clicking on specific points on the map will make a pop message appear which displays information such as site name, address, and phone number.

Usability Testing

In the time that we had, our team conducted one round of usability testing with five different users on our paper prototype. I had test participants perform two tasks related to the filter categories and site info features. At the end of each test, we had users fill out a five question likert survey to gauge the app's intuitiveness.

Test Findings

We discovered through our findings that all of our test participants felt it was easy to use. However, two of our users struggled with deciphering what the icons meant in the category filters given the lack of labels. Users also mentioned that the user location icon on the map being too big which could obstruct sites of interest.

Design Iterations

Our team iterated on the designs to address user concerns from our test findings. Our next design iterations included labelling of the filter categories to make them more identifiable. Other changes included reducing the size of the user location icon to not block points of interest on the map.

Final Product

Our team struggled to find the APIs for resource locations and had to resort to hardcoding the points of interest instead. This was due to the fact that not many APIs or data exist out there for homeless shelters. As a result, the final product ended up being more proof of concept than a functioning app.

Next Steps

The next step would be to test and iterate designs on additional features like the search bar, custom filters, resource voting, entry, and validation. We would also like to include additional categories and sites of interest such as men and women's shelter, domestic violence resources, veteran's aid, etc. In addition, we would also need to schedule time to test our designs with target users as soon as possible. Finally, our developers will need to search for APIs that would work for our app as we did not use one.

Learnings

Knowing how to pivot during any time during a project is important as project parameters constantly change. Asking questions and being vocal with your team helps communicate ideas and gets the ball rolling. Being able to decide early on what the team wants to accomplish versus what needs to be completed means less time deliberating and more time doing.
