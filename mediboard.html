Project Details

My Role: UX Designer

Duration: 3 Months

Tools: Figma, Webflow, Notion, Card Sorting

Background

Medical information can be unorganized, out-of-date, and difficult to digest. MediBoard attempts to solve this problem with a data visualization tool to quantitatively compare medical treatments against conditions like depression, anxiety, and insomnia using real and up-to-date clinical trial data. As the designer, I conducted research, iterated & tested designs, and facilitated development of the app with developers.

Preliminary Research

Our team looked into the business viability of the visualization tool by exploring existing medical databases and registries such as ClinicalTrials.gov and National Institutes of Health. In addition, we spoke to multiple people on social media sites and network groups like Reddit and Facebook to see if there was a market for the visualization tool.

Competitive Analysis

We discovered that many sites out there did not conduct meta-analysis on the studies for accuracy, allow for comparing treaments to see which options were better, or enable people to discuss and share their conditions or experiences.

User Stories

Our team drafted three different user stories that aligned with the most common responses from the feedback we received. This exercise would help us ideate and arrive at the optimal solution for each problem.



I would like to know the treatment options for a condition:

  • User visits MediBoard
  • Searches for condition
  • Visits the condition page
  • Looks at all the possible treatments
  • Looks at recommendations for user posts
  • Compares treatments side-by-side


I would like to know the alternatives to this treatment:

  • User visits MediBoard
  • Searches for treatment
  • Looks at the alternatives treatments section
  • Browse and starts to compare them
  • Comes up with a few they like


I would like to connect with others who share their conditions:

  • User visits MediBoard
  • Searches for their treatment or uses the message board tags
  • Browses post

Persona Development

Our team came up with two different personas that encapsulate our model users. Rebecca is a researcher who uses medical data to do her job. Shyla is a patient who is looking for the right treatment options for her condition.

Problem

The goal of every medical site is to inform people. However, people need up-to-date and trusted health information so they can make the best health decisions. For patients and researchers, the main concern is the accuracy and reliability of information. People need accurate medical information because they become frustrated if they are unable to obtain the right information.

Solution

We believe implementing a data visualization tool that allows users to get accurate and reliable information will create a pleasant searching experience. We will know this to be true when we see an increase in satisfaction and ratings.

Design Ideation & Sketches

Early concept sketches of the visualization tool consisted of the Studies and Compare Treatments pages. The Studies page is primarily for comparing and analyzing treatments on a granular level while the Compare Treatments page is geared toward finding information and treatment for a condition.

The Study page is intended to give users a granular view of the information in the study.

Compare Treatments

The Compare Treatments page's purpose is to allow users to learn more about the condition, compare different treatment options, and to learn people's experience with them.

Usability Testing & Findings

Our team conducted usability testings on the Studies and Compare Treatment pages with three average users and two researchers. In addition, I also conducted a SUS Survey to calculate the usability score.



Findings: A major frustration point for users was the amount of information on the page. Average users stated they only are interested in the treatment, safety, survival, and side effects. The research users mentioned that the information makes them think too hard.

Design Iterations & Wireframes

Our team iterated on the designs to address user concerns from our test findings. Our next design iterations included labelling of the filter categories to make them more identifiable. Other changes included reducing the size of the user location icon to not block points of interest on the map.

Learnings

Knowing how to pivot during any time during a project is important as project parameters constantly change. Asking questions and being vocal with your team helps communicate ideas and gets the ball rolling. Being able to decide early on what the team wants to accomplish versus what needs to be completed means less time deliberating and more time doing.
