Interviews and Insights

When I went to my client site (Pink Gorilla Games has two locations), I realized that my questions were too broad and had very little to do with their online presence. In retrospect, I should have tailored my questions more toward this. Regardless, I ended up creating an affinity diagram and one of the things I discovered was that many people appreciated how organized and clean the store was. I looked at designs and features from other websites like Gamestop, Amazon, and Newegg. One important insight that I discovered from my competitive analysis was that the website lacked any way of viewing or writing a review of their product.

Persona and Context Scenario

The persona that I chose to do was John Goodson. He was a tech savvy art teacher who wanted to find the right activities for his daughter. John Goodson’s key needs from the store included having quick access to a range of options, knowing what’s new on repeat visits, and a feeling of relationship with the brand. Pain points included difficult navigation, lack of sufficient product descriptions, and lack of trust with unfamiliar brands. Based on information about his persona and from my synthesis, I created a context scenario where John needed to find a reputable video game retailer where he could see the reviews of games on the e-commerce site and purchase and return games easily.

Design and Iteration

I decided to redesign their e-commerce store to make it easily navigable and organized like how the store was. I also decided to incorporate a feature to view and write reviews in my website redesign. One of the first things I did was redesign the sitemap of Pink Gorilla Game’s website. The current sitemap was messy and disorganized. It was not immediately apparent what the mission statement was and what the website was trying to do. In addition, some links from the side nav had nothing. After talking with the store owner, I determined that Pink Gorilla Games is all about Buying, Selling, and Trading. I made them the primary links on the home page. I put home, about, contact us, and events as the secondary nav in the top right corner. Everything else like FAQ, Return Policy, etc. I put in the footer. The end result is a site map that is more streamlined and less redundant.

Prototyping and Usability Test

I conducted 5 usability tests with 5 different users. I tasked them with checking out and purchasing a game from the website. Every user successfully performed this task, but had feedback about the cosmetic details of the site. These include: Making the menu catalog window bigger Relocating social media icons Expand on the shipping and billing options Needs more validation when an item was added to the cart (current problem was that updating the cart with a number next to it wasn’t enough to convey to the user that an item was added) I addressed all these on my 2nd iteration by first making the catalog menu bigger. I moved the social media icons in the footer. I included additional shipping options in the shipping and payment screen. To address with validation, when the user added an item to the cart, it would take them directly to the shopping cart.

Conclusion

As a result of spending too much time on my wireframes, I had only done one round of usability testing and two iterations of my designs. In retrospect, I should have spent more time conducting usability tests and less time worrying about the details of the wireframes. What was not shown during my presentation was that I actually did some sketches in Balsamiq. I think I should’ve also conducted usability tests with paper prototypes and Balsamiq earlier.

Next Steps

The next steps of this project would be to create sketches for Trade and Sell options on the website. Also on the product and detailed product pages, I would need to figure out and test with users whether it would be a good idea to have the ratings on the product page or detailed product pages.
