Introduction

As part of a project at General Assembly, I worked as the UX researcher focusing on redesigning the Puget Sound Trip Planner mobile app. I worked together with the Project Manager and Interaction Designer to create an interactive prototype of the redesign.

Problem

The most glaring problem was the non-intuitive interface of the app. This was the main focal point of our redesign. In addition, some of the features on the app were not even functional such as adding routes to your favorites list.

Solution

Puget Sound Trip Planner was completely redone. Content and information was reorganized to meet our user’s needs. Emphasis was put on structuring content around Trip Planner’s alerts feature and making it more intuitive.

Strategic Analysis

Users suggested that Puget Sound Trip Planner would be more intuitive if it followed some design patterns of their favorite apps. In response to this, my team took a look at competitors app layouts and paid close attention to the apps mentioned in user interviews (Google, Apple, and One Bus Away). My discovery was that these more popular apps had a map on the home screen as well as minimal navigation. Puget Sound Trip Planner has an unwelcoming interface and is not intuitive to use. The app has a map which allows to find your destination relative to where you are, but it took some additional navigation to get to that feature.

Research

To find out better who my users were and what their needs and wants were, we went out and asked people near bus stops and public locations about what kind of mobile transportation app they used. These people ranged from students to working adults. Based on the research we collected, we discovered 6 out of 7 people we tested used 2 or more transportation apps for specific purposes. For example, people used Google Maps because it is easy and intuitive to find directions. However, at an actual bus stop, people would utilize One Bus Away because it provided them with real time information of when the bus would arrive. Google Maps does not always provide reliable or real time information.

Conclusion

I thought that we should have started our first design iterations on paper or at low fidelity even though I understood the argument of making things more detailed to assist with user guidance. We jumped to mid-fidelity using Sketch and inVision. As a result, this made it harder to pin-point issues that could’ve been related to the core of the design rather than the specific features. Because we started with a mid fidelity design, we were confined to it. I felt that was evidently the case during our three usability tests. The feedback we received from the users was largely inconsistent. I also should have interviewed people that used Puget Sound Trip Planner. While I tried to see if the people I interviewed had used this specific app before during my user interviews, I admit I did not exercise all communication channels to see if other people had used Puget Sound Trip Planner app. This was largely due to the time. This resulted in borrowing design ideas and getting inspiration from other apps rather than focusing on what to specifically fix based on the existing Puget Sound Trip Planner app. I think we did a good job with our presentation in terms tying in our user persona. We constantly communicated what we were going to do and delivered a prototype that was extremely detailed.
